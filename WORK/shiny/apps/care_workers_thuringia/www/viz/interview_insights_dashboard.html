Key Finding
The interview data reveals a care worker recruitment system in Thuringia that is operationally sophisticated but strategically reactive. Agencies demonstrate remarkable commitment to supporting migrant workers after arrival, yet the data suggests insufficient investment in pre-arrival preparation and structural solutions to persistent challenges.

The Language Barrier - Deep Dive 🗣️

Language emerges as the dominant challenge, appearing in over one-third of all coded entries.

  • B1 level is considered insufficient for nursing training
  • B2 level is the minimum threshold for success
  • Language difficulties compound other challenges
  • Agencies invest significant resources in language support

"Because experience has shown that entering with B1 or lower makes it too difficult for the young person during the intensive and challenging generalist training to become a nursing specialist."

— Interview Extract

Xenophobia and Discrimination 🚨
The Xenophobia Paradox
Despite appearing in only 3.2% of entries explicitly, xenophobia (C08) is the second most frequent code at 10.4%. Agencies discuss xenophobia frequently but frame it as external to their control, a regional characteristic, and something workers must be "prepared for" rather than protected from.

"We still try to be neutral... But you still prepare people for the countryside, where it might happen—even in the city. You're no longer protected anywhere."

— Interview Extract

Key Narratives from the Data

Representative quotes and themes

The Complexity of Transnational Coordination 🌐

"And this is especially a highly complex process in connection with foreign embassies, German schools, Goethe Institutes, and so on. Everything has to be coordinated properly."

Recruitment involves multiple actors across borders, requiring sophisticated coordination.

The Expectation Management Challenge 🎯

"We explain what the training involves... We dispel that notion relatively early and tell people if you want to go to a hospital, then this isn't the right thing for you."

Agencies actively work to align worker expectations with German realities.

The Support Beyond Duty 💪

"They also wanted to network, or they wanted a bicycle—I then organized that via eBay classifieds—such small things."

Support extends far beyond formal responsibilities into personal assistance.

The Unresolved Tensions 😔

"But if that can't resolve it, then unfortunately there are young people who move away... That's just how it is."

Some challenges are acknowledged but accepted as unchangeable.

Strategic Insights

Strengths, gaps, and opportunities

Strengths of the Current System ✅
✓Robust support infrastructure once workers arrive
✓Strong inter-organizational cooperation (Goethe, ZAV, LEG)
✓Diverse source country portfolio reducing dependency
✓Active expectation management before arrival
✓Universal recognition of key challenges across all stakeholders
Gaps and Vulnerabilities ⚠️
✗Reactive orientation – more resources on post-arrival than prevention
✗Language barrier remains critical despite awareness
✗Xenophobia acknowledged but not actively countered
✗Qualification recognition underaddressed (only 4.8%)
✗Retention challenges accepted rather than solved
✗Pre-arrival preparation insufficient relative to post-arrival support
10

Conclusion

Key takeaways and recommendations

The Central Paradox
Organizations acknowledge xenophobia, retention problems, and qualification mismatches as significant issues, yet frame them as external constraints rather than problems requiring systemic intervention.
The Opportunity
Shifting resources toward pre-arrival preparation, language threshold enforcement, and proactive anti-discrimination measures could reduce the burden of post-arrival support while improving worker outcomes.
